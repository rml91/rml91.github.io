Research

Conformal Gravity & Cosmological implications

We investigate the cosmological predictions of the Conformal Higgs Model (CHM) proposed by Nesbet (2023), with particular focus on relaxing the static Higgs field assumption $\phi(t) = \phi_0$ that underlies the original analysis. We first reproduce Nesbet's Table I luminosity distance results and verify the conformal sum rule $\Omega_\Lambda + \Omega_q = 1$ for redshifts $z \leq 1$, confirming agreement with the standard $\Lambda$CDM fit using a single free parameter $\alpha = \Omega_\Lambda(t_0) = 0.732$. We then evaluate the Ricci scalar $R(z)$ and introduce a diagnostic $D(z)$ to quantify the relative importance of time-derivative terms in the scalar field equation, establishing the redshift at which Nesbet's static approximation breaks down. Finally, we solve the fully coupled dynamical system for the scale factor $a(t)$ and Higgs field $\phi(t)$ simultaneously, without assuming $\phi(t) = \phi_0$, by implementing the gauge-field dressing condition $w^2(z) = (1+z)^2 H^2 (\phi'/\phi)^2$ self-consistently. The resulting dynamical CHM predictions for $H(z)$, the density parameters $\Omega_\Lambda(z)$, $\Omega_q(z)$, and the luminosity distance $H_0 d_L/c$ are compared against both the Nesbet static approximation and the standard $\Lambda$CDM model. Our results quantify the corrections to conformal gravity cosmology arising from Higgs field dynamics and extend the validity of CHM predictions to higher redshifts beyond $z \leq 1$ (hopefully).

Supervision: Prof. Keith Horne

Formalisation of electrical networks for the doubling map

Classical results show that for finite, reversible Markov chains, probabilistic quantities expressed via electrical analogies: voltages, currents, and effective resistances. We extend these methods to Markov partitions of dynamical systems encoded as topological Markov chains. De Bruijn graphs can model these sequences equipped with non-uniform Bernoulli measures. We transform these irreversible processes into electrical networks, building on the work of Doyle-Snell and Chandra for probabilistic interpretations, Gaudillière-Landim-Slowik for the extension of the Dirichlet principle, and finally Balasz-Folly for their amplifiers. This is done by perturbing (and renormalising) the transition probabilities and computing the commute times before taking the limit of the perturbation to zero. The effective resistance will be used to define a metric corresponding to a dynamical distance on the discrete space state of linear expanding maps of the circle, in particular, the doubling map.

Supervision: Dr. Natalia Jurga
Funding: The Carnegie Trust and StARIS Scheme

Modelling few-emitter lasing with organic molecules (Incoming)

Strong coupling between organic molecules and electromagnetic radiation has been extensively studied in the last decade, as a way to engineer polaritonic effects in systems that are relatively cheap to produce. One recent set of experiments has studied the behaviour of a small number of molecules coupling to plasmonic nanocavities. This leads to an odd regime of lasing, as the number of emitters is small, the "cavity" has a large linewidth, but the coupling to each emitter is relatively strong. These experiments were compared to a theoretical model based on two-level emitters coupled to a cavity mode, and studied using approaches based on cumulant approximations. The aim of this project is to consider a more detailed model of molecules, accounting for vibrational modes, and to perform exact calculations based on the limit of very small numbers of molecules coupled to one cavity. We will consider the Holstein-Tavis-Cummings model to describe vibrationally dressed emitters, accounting for relaxation processes including thermalisation of vibrational modes. We will then study this model using numerical methods that make use of (weak) permutational symmetry among the emitters. The aims of the project are (a) to see what scale of system can be studied computationally using this approach, and (b) to understand how the inclusion of vibrational modes change the behaviour found in, particularly the emission spectrum.

Supervision: Prof. Jonathan Keeling & Prof. Brendon Lovett
Funding: SSC Council (St Andrews)
